CONSISTENT CUTPINNS FOR CONVECTION-DIFFUSION EQUATIONS ON CURVED LEVEL-SET DOMAINS 

MANEESH KUMAR SINGH 

Abstract. We present an a priori error analysis of consistent-loss PINNs for stationary convection-diffusion equations on curved level-set domains. The standard mean-squared interior loss fails in the convection-dominated regime: the solution develops an O(ε) boundary layer in which the pointwise residual grows like ε−1, so the loss is dominated by the few collocation points inside the layer and leaves the smooth bulk unresolved. We remove this mismatch by penalising the interior residual in a discrete Lγ norm with γ = 1 + 1 / log ˜ m, a computable surrogate for the 

H−1 stability term, and imposing the boundary condition through a discrete H1/2(∂Ω) trace norm, which treats flat and curved geometries uniformly. Under Besov regularity assumptions we prove a single a priori H1 error bound, valid for all interior exponents γ ∈ (1 , 2] , with an optimal recovery rate governed by a cut-cell floor 1/(2 γ) specific to the curved geometry. Nu-merical experiments on a rectangle and a disk at ε = 2 −s, s ∈ { 2, 4, 6}, confirm the analysis: as the layer sharpens, the L2 interior loss becomes seed-fragile while the Lγ interior trains reliably, the interior norm being the decisive factor in convergence. 

1. Introduction 

1.1. Background and motivation. The stationary convection-diffusion equation (1) −ε∆u + b · ∇ u + c u = f in Ω, u = g on ∂Ω,

on a bounded domain Ω ⊂ R2 underlies the modelling of many transport phenomena, from the spread of a pollutant and charge transport in semiconductors to the Oseen system obtained by linearising incompressible flow. How the solution behaves is set by the ratio of the diffusion coefficient ε to the convection magnitude ∥b∥∞. When that ratio becomes small, the regime we call convection-dominated, u develops thin layers near the boundary or in the interior, and approximating it accurately has long been difficult for classical discretisations [30, 33, 34] and for neural-network solvers [14, 18] alike. Among neural-network approaches, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) [29] hold par-ticular appeal here: because the governing equation is enforced at scattered points rather than over a triangulation, no mesh is required, which spares the cost of building and repeatedly refin-ing a conforming grid on a geometrically intricate domain. The standard formulation nonetheless meets two separate obstacles in the present setting. The first is a question of norms. On a gen-eral bounded domain the L2(∂Ω) boundary penalty fails to control the H1/2(∂Ω) trace norm that governs well-posedness in H1(Ω) , so a loss built on it is not consistent with the functional-analytic structure of the problem. The second is specific to convection dominance. Writing the pointwise residual as R(v)( x) := −ε∆v +b·∇ v +cv −f , its values reach order ε−1 throughout the 

O(ε)-wide layer, so the few collocation points of X that land there dominate the squared-residual sum. Minimising such a loss drives the network to resolve the layer while neglecting the smooth bulk, which is exactly where the H1 error is concentrated. The idea of training a network to satisfy a differential equation predates the modern formula-tion [20], but it is the collocation-based variant of [29] that has driven the recent activity, surveyed in [8, 27]. A substantial theory has since accumulated. Convergence for linear second-order el-liptic and parabolic equations was established in [31], generalisation and approximation error

Prior work and the open intersection. Now, we discussed the previous work related to Consistent PINN and the motivation behind this research. 

Consistent PINN losses on flat domains: The norm mismatch on the boundary was first resolved by Bonito, DeVore, Petrova, and Siegel [4] for the unit cube. In place of the L2(∂Ω) 

penalty they introduced a discrete Gagliardo double sum over the boundary collocation points, and they showed this discrete functional to be equivalent to the continuous H1/2(∂Ω) norm over suitable Besov model classes. A loss assembled from it inherits provable H1 recovery rates. Their treatment was confined to elliptic problems on flat geometry, and the interior residual was always measured in L2.

Consistent losses on curved cut domains: Carrying the consistent framework onto curved geometry, Singh [32] treated bounded domains given as the negative level set of a C2 function. The technical heart of that work is a chord-arc lemma establishing that the discrete Gagliardo seminorm, evaluated on m points spaced equally in arc length along a C2 Jordan curve, stays equivalent to the continuous H1/2(∂Ω) trace norm; the resulting H1 rates match those of [4] up to constants set by the curvature and the chord-arc parameter. Two interior penalties were in fact defined there, one using Lγ with γ = 1 + 1 / log ˜ m and one using L2, but on the smooth elliptic benchmarks of that paper the two choices returned all but identical H1 error, and the theory and the experiments of that paper were both developed for the L2 penalty. What was an immaterial modelling choice in the elliptic case is, as we show below, the deciding factor once convection dominates. 

PINNs for convection-dominated problems: The instability of standard PINNs on convection-dominated PDEs was documented by Krishnapriyan et al. [18], who linked it to pathologies of the optimisation landscape near the layer. The responses in the literature pull in different directions: coupling a PINN to an SUPG-stabilised finite element reference [6]; neural prediction of stabilisation parameters for convection-dominated problems [37, 38]; hp -variational PINNs in one dimension or on rectangles [1, 2, 19]; direct PINN treatment of boundary-layer problems [28]; reweighting the loss and adapting the sample distribution [14]; and unfitted finite-element neural schemes carrying a CutFEM-type test space [21], built on aggregated unfitted finite element technology [3]. Individually these target convection, geometry, or loss design, yet none unites a consistent-loss formulation with a curved cut domain and a convection-dominated regime while remaining a pure PINN. 

Consistent PINN for convective operators: The consistent-loss programme has by now been carried to higher-order elliptic operators [22] and to obstacle problems in mixed form [17], but its only treatment of a convective operator to date is the consistent-PINN study of the stationary Oseen system by Mishra and Khan [23], which derives its loss from the stability structure of a convective problem and proves optimal recovery in H1 for the velocity together with L2 for the pressure. We share its premise, a loss read off the continuous stability estimate of a problem carrying a first-order term, and it confirms that the optimal-recovery apparatus of [4] survives the passage to such operators. Two features separate it from the layer-dominated setting we study. Its analysis and numerics sit in the balanced regime, with unit viscosity ν = 1 on the unit square, giving a Péclet number of order one, smooth layer-free solutions, and an L2 interior penalty throughout; the overweighting mechanism we are concerned with simply does not occur there, and nothing forces a departure from L2. Its geometry is moreover a flat box, so cut cells CONSISTENT CUTPINNS FOR CONVECTION-DIFFUSION 3

and curved-boundary trace equivalence never enter. Pushing the consistent loss into convection dominance forces the interior norm to change, and pushing it onto curved cut domains brings in the trace machinery of [32]; the Lγ handling of a convective residual that [23] carries out at the 

L2 level is, here, precisely the part that must be reworked rather than reused. 

The open intersection. Two bodies of work thus stop short of meeting. On the consistent-loss side, [4] covers only Poisson on flat geometry and [32] only Poisson on cut domains; on the convection-diffusion PINN side, [2, 6, 14, 19] remain on flat or rectangular domains and out-side the consistent-loss paradigm, relying instead on FEM coupling, Petrov–Galerkin variational forms, or hand-tuned reweighting. A consistent-PINN account of convection-diffusion appears not to have been given even on a rectangle, and the curved cut-domain case is open as well. The present paper supplies both at once. 1.3. Contributions. The main contribution of our work is listed below. (1) First consistent-loss treatment of convection-diffusion. We are not aware of any prior study that places the stationary convection-diffusion equation inside the consistent-PINN framework, on either flat or curved geometry. Existing consistent-loss work treats Poisson-type problems only [4, 32], while the convection-diffusion PINN literature [2, 6, 14, 19] operates outside the consistent-loss paradigm, using SUPG-stabilised hybrids, Petrov–Galerkin variational PINNs, or empirical loss reweighting. The present work occupies this intersection. (2) A norm comparison identifying the interior norm as the governing choice. We compare three losses on a common ansatz: standard PINN ( L2 interior, L2 boundary); the consistent loss ( L2 interior, discrete H1/2(∂Ω) boundary); and the Lγ loss ( Lγ interior with γ = 1 + 1 / log ˜ m, discrete H1/2(∂Ω) boundary), across a rectangle and a disk at ε =2−s, s ∈ { 2, 4, 6}. The comparison shows that, in the convection-dominated regime, the consistent boundary norm alone is not enough, and that the correction that matters is in the interior norm. The exponent γ = 1+1 / log ˜ m is the H−1-side recovery exponent of [4], which we find transfers to the convection-dominated setting; since it is fixed a priori by the interior budget ˜m, it leaves the method with no interior-norm tuning parameter. On seeds that converge, the three losses are comparable in H1 accuracy, and the separation between them is in reliability . As the layer sharpens, the L2-interior losses become seed-fragile and diverge on a growing fraction of initialisations, whereas Lγ remains dependable across every seed on both domains. The interior norm governs whether convection-dominated training converges at all, not only the accuracy of a converged run. (3) An a priori H1 error bound. We prove an a priori bound for the consistent CutPINN approximation of (1) of the form

Remark 2.1. The regime of interest in this work is convection-dominated but not singularly perturbed in the asymptotic sense. Here ε ranges over 2−s for s ∈ { 2, 4, 6}, giving a global Péclet number |b|L/ε of order ∼ 4 to ∼ 60 , which is solidly convection-dominated. At the budget used in the experiments the mesh Péclet |b|h/ε with h ∼ ˜m−1/2 stays O(1) , so the O(ε)-wide layer is 

marginally resolved by the collocation set, with on the order of ˜mε points falling in it. In this regime the failure of the L2 interior loss is one of overweighting a layer that is resolved, not of failing to see an unresolved one, and it is this overweighting that the Lγ correction addresses; see Remark 3.3. Remark 3.2 (Why γ = 1 + 1 / log ˜ m). The choice of γ is dictated by the optimal recovery theory of [4], where it arises as the boundary between two regimes in the recovery exponent on Besov model classes. Concretely, the Lγ norm interpolates between L1 and L2 in such a way that the discrete Lγ functional remains equivalent to the continuous Lγ norm on the model class 

U (Bsγ (Lγ (Ω))) , with s matching the smoothness of the source f , while the Lγ norm in turn controls the H−1 residual through the Sobolev embedding used in the analysis below. Ongoing and Future Work. Several directions extend naturally from here. The a priori bound (23) carries the convection-diffusion stability constant ε−1, and sharpening it to an ε-uniform estimate in the singular-perturbation sense is within reach through a streamline-diffusion stability analysis on the cut domain, together with a finer treatment of the boundary chord-arc constants near the outflow arc. It would also be interesting to study the singularly perturbed regime, where the layer sharpens further and adaptive collocation, complementary to the interior-norm correction studied here, becomes relevant. The Lγ interior already matches or surpasses several established enhancements in our experiments, including SUPG-style residual reweight-ing [5], anisotropic outflow collocation, and Fourier-feature embeddings; mapping out the prob-lem classes on which each of these could reinforce, rather than compete with, the consistent loss is a worthwhile direction in its own right. The framework also points beyond the stationary problem. The spatial discretisation devel-oped here transfers directly to the parabolic convection-diffusion equation

under a backward-Euler discretisation in time, in which each step is a stationary problem of the form (3) with reaction coefficient c + (∆ t)−1, precisely the setting analysed here. This time-dependent extension is the subject of ongoing work.
