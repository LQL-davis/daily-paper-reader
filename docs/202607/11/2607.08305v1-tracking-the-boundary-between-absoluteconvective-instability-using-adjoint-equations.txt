TRACKING THE BOUNDARY BETWEEN ABSOLUTE/CONVECTIVE INSTABILITY USING ADJOINT EQUATIONS ∗

YUE XIAO 1 , HUI LI 2,3 , AND ZIJING DING 2,3,* 

Abstract. Determining absolute/convective instability boundaries conventionally requires re-peated saddle searches in the complex-wavenumber plane and a subsequent scan of the physical parameter space to locate zero absolute growth. Such nested calculations become costly and sensi-tive to modal branch association for large non-normal eigenvalue problems. This work develops a direct continuation method for neutral stationary-saddle boundaries of frequency-affine generalised eigenvalue problems. The zero-group-velocity condition is expressed as an adjoint solvability resid-ual and solved together with the direct and adjoint eigenproblems, complex gauge constraints and the neutral-growth condition. The resulting one-dimensional solution manifold in the combined state–parameter space is tracked by scaled pseudo-arclength continuation, allowing parameter folds to be crossed without switching the physical continuation variable. The formulation recovers the analytical Ginzburg–Landau boundary and, for a Gaussian-wake Orr–Sommerfeld problem, agrees with separately formulated finite-difference saddle corrections to approximately 10 −8 in relative crit-ical Reynolds number. Compared with nested complex-wavenumber and parameter-plane saddle scanning, the scanning calculations require 14 .0–30 .6 times the wall time of the direct adjoint contin-uation, with the cost increasing as the reconstructed boundary is refined. Application to a coupled Oldroyd–B free-surface film reveals genuine folds of the neutral-saddle manifold and a re-entrant CI– AI–CI boundary geometry for the selected saddle family. The results show that adjoint-augmented pseudo-arclength continuation can replace nested saddle searches and parameter-plane reconstruction by direct and computationally efficient tracking of the neutral boundary itself. 

Key words. Adjoint method, Pseudo-arclength continuation, Saddle point, Spatio-temporal instability 

1. Introduction. The response of an open flow to a localised disturbance is governed not only by temporal amplification but also by the way disturbances prop-agate through the domain. In spatio-temporal stability theory, the transition from convective to absolute instability is associated with a branch-point singularity of the dispersion relation in the complex wavenumber plane and, for the physical transition, with the pinching of spatial branches of opposite spatial causality [1, 2]. With the normal-mode convention (1.1) q′(x, y, t ) = ˆ q(y) exp[i( kx − ωt )] ,

a neutral absolute/convective instability boundary is characterised algebraically by (1.2) dω

dk = 0 , Im( ω) = 0 ,

provided that the stationary point is the relevant Briggs–Bers pinch. A direct numerical implementation of this definition is often cumbersome. A con-ventional workflow first solves a temporal or spatial eigenvalue problem at many neigh-bouring complex wavenumbers, associates eigenvalues with a selected modal branch, approximates d ω/ dk by finite differences, iterates for a saddle point, and finally em-beds that saddle search in an outer parameter loop to locate zero absolute growth. The procedure becomes particularly delicate when eigenvalue branches approach one another, because small branch-association errors contaminate the numerical deriv-ative. It is also expensive when each eigenvalue solve involves a large multi-field discretisation. Adjoint solvability provides an alternative. For non-self-adjoint eigenvalue prob-lems, direct–adjoint identities can evaluate eigenvalue sensitivities without explicitly differentiating the eigenvector or repeatedly resolving neighbouring eigenvalues; re-lated ideas are widely used in modal sensitivity analysis [5]. In the present work, the adjoint identity is used differently: the zero-group-velocity condition itself is inserted into a coupled nonlinear system and solved simultaneously with the direct and adjoint eigenproblems. The neutral saddle is therefore treated as a solution of an extended algebraic system rather than as the result of a nested eigenvalue-differentiation and

This viewpoint is important computationally: rather than evaluating saddle growth rates throughout a two-dimensional parameter plane and reconstructing the neutral contour afterwards, the numerical method advances directly along the bound-ary of interest. It is also important geometrically, because a fold in the projection onto either physical parameter does not terminate the underlying solution manifold. The resulting framework combines four numerical ingredients. First, the station-ary condition is imposed through an adjoint algebraic residual, avoiding repeated finite-difference differentiation of a tracked temporal eigenvalue branch within the boundary equations. Second, two complex gauge conditions and a reduced adjoint equation remove the direct- and adjoint-mode scale and phase freedoms and produce a square fixed-parameter Newton system. Third, after one neutral saddle has been corrected, the fixed-parameter constraint is removed and the resulting underdeter-mined system is followed by scaled two-sided pseudo-arclength continuation. This permits the same formulation to remain regular when a physical continuation param-eter loses local uniqueness. Fourth, the large direct- and adjoint-mode blocks of the Newton Jacobian are assembled from exact first variations, while only the scalar fre-quency, wavenumber and control-parameter columns are evaluated numerically. The method therefore targets the two principal numerical costs of conventional boundary construction: repeated complex-wavenumber saddle searches and repeated sampling of the surrounding physical parameter plane. The formulation is assessed at three levels. The linear Ginzburg–Landau equa-tion provides an exact scalar neutral-saddle manifold and verifies the reduction of the general adjoint system. A planar Gaussian wake then provides a non-normal Orr– Sommerfeld test: local finite-difference corrections cross-validate the continued saddle states, representative spatial-root calculations verify the corresponding pinch topol-ogy, and a nested complex-wavenumber/parameter-plane scanning benchmark quan-tifies the cost of reconstructing the same boundary indirectly. Finally, the framework is applied without a film-specific continuation reformulation to a coupled Oldroyd– B free-surface eigenproblem containing hydrodynamic, constitutive and interfacial unknowns. The film calculation provides a genuine fold test, for which natural con-tinuation in the Reynolds number loses local solvability whereas pseudo-arclength continuation crosses the turning point and reveals the post-fold branch of the selected neutral-saddle family. Section 2 develops the adjoint and continuation formulation. Sections 3 and 4 present the analytical and Gaussian-wake assessments, respectively. Section 5 considers the viscoelastic-film application, and the principal findings and limitations are summarised in Sec. 6.

4.3. Computational efficiency relative to Briggs nested saddle scan-ning. The finite-difference calculations in the preceding subsection are intended as local cross-validation of selected neutral-saddle states and do not constitute an inde-pendent boundary-construction procedure. To assess the cost of constructing the complete boundary, the direct adjoint trace is compared with a nested complex-wavenumber and parameter-plane saddle-scanning procedure. The two approaches use the same mapped Orr–Sommerfeld discretisation, Eq. (4.7), and target the saddle family identified from the neutral point at λ = 0 .95. No points from the computed adjoint boundary are supplied to the scanning calculation. At each sampled parameter pair ( Re, λ ), a temporal eigenvalue sheet is first followed on a fixed 11 ×11 complex-α grid covering 0 .25 ≤ αr ≤ 1.45 , and −1.55 ≤ αi ≤ − 0.15 . Candidate stationary points are detected from small values of the finite-difference group velocity 

gFD (α) = ω(α+hα)−ω(α−hα)2hα , and are locally refined by solving Re gFD = Im gFD = 0 with a finite-difference Newton iteration in ( αr , α i). Continuity in the saddle coordi-nates, eigenvalue and modal overlap is used to retain the selected saddle family. The absolute growth rate (4.16) γ(Re, λ ) = Im ωs(Re, λ )is then evaluated throughout a two-dimensional parameter grid, and the neutral boundary is reconstructed a posteriori from the zero contour γ = 0. The compar-ison therefore represents a nested scanning-and-reconstruction workflow rather than an optimally warm-started saddle-continuation algorithm. 5.4. Effect of elasticity and inclination on the AI/CI transition. The identification of the smaller-Re side as absolutely unstable gives the boundary ge-ometry a direct physical interpretation. Starting from De = 0, the critical Reynolds number initially increases with De and reaches Re F at the fold, see Fig. 7(a). The ab-solutely unstable region therefore expands toward larger Reynolds numbers as weak-to-moderate elasticity is introduced. Beyond the fold, Re c decreases with further increase of De , so the trend reverses and the absolutely unstable region contracts. The fold also implies a re-entrant transition at fixed Reynolds number. For a given inclination and Re c(0) < Re < Re F , a vertical line in the ( Re, De ) plane in-tersects the connected neutral-saddle branch twice. Because the smaller-Re side is absolutely unstable, increasing De at fixed Re produces CI–AI–CI transition. Elas-ticity therefore does not act as a uniformly stabilising or destabilising parameter in the spatio-temporal problem. Instead, it first promotes the absolute mode over the pre-fold segment and suppresses it again on the post-fold segment. The present cal-culations establish the resulting shift of the selected neutral saddle and the re-entrant AI/CI geometry. A detailed energetic or stress-budget decomposition of the underly-ing viscoelastic mechanism is outside the scope of the continuation study and would require a separate modal-energy analysis. 

6. Conclusions. A direct adjoint-augmented continuation framework has been developed for tracking neutral stationary-saddle boundaries in frequency-affine gen-eralised eigenvalue problems. The central idea is to embed the zero-group-velocity condition in the nonlinear eigenvalue system through an adjoint solvability resid-24 Y. XIAO, H. LI, AND Z. DING 

ual. The direct and adjoint eigenproblems, the stationarity condition, the eigenvector gauges and the neutral-growth condition are thereby solved as a single augmented system. When two physical parameters are allowed to vary, the neutral saddles are represented directly as a one-dimensional solution manifold and followed by scaled pseudo-arclength continuation. This formulation changes the numerical object being computed. Conventional boundary reconstruction commonly requires a saddle search over the complex wavenum-ber plane at many parameter values, followed by an additional search or contour ex-traction in the physical parameter plane. The present approach instead advances di-rectly along the neutral-saddle manifold. The Gaussian-wake calculations show that the adjoint formulation is consistent with a separately formulated finite-difference saddle residual and recovers the spatial pinch topology of the selected mode. The comparison with nested saddle scanning further demonstrates the computational con-sequence of the manifold formulation: increasing the resolution of a parameter-plane scan improves the reconstructed boundary only by repeating the underlying spectral and saddle calculations at additional parameter points, whereas pseudo-arclength con-tinuation concentrates the computation on the boundary itself. The Oldroyd–B film problem demonstrates that the same framework extends to a coupled multi-field free-surface eigenproblem without a problem-specific continuation reformulation. The continued neutral-saddle manifolds develop genuine folds in their Reynolds-number projections. Near such a fold, natural continuation in the Reynolds number loses local solvability, whereas pseudo-arclength continuation remains regular and resolves the post-fold branch. For the selected saddle family examined here, the resulting ( Re, De ) boundary geometry shows that elasticity does not shift the neu-tral boundary monotonically: the pre- and post-fold branches produce a re-entrant CI–AI–CI sequence over part of the parameter range. The film application therefore illustrates both the numerical role of the pseudo-arclength constraint and the addi-tional boundary structure that may be missed when a single physical parameter is imposed as the continuation coordinate. The present method is a correction and continuation framework for a selected saddle family. It assumes a locally simple finite temporal eigenvalue and a regular one-dimensional augmented solution manifold, and it requires an approximate initial saddle and modal-family identity. Moreover, the algebraic conditions d ω/ dk = 0 and Im ω = 0 identify neutral stationary eigenpairs but do not by themselves establish Briggs–Bers pinching or dominance over competing saddle families. Spatial-branch topology and possible changes in saddle dominance must therefore be examined sep-arately when required by the physical interpretation. Subject to these qualifications, the results establish adjoint-augmented pseudo-arclength continuation as a direct route for computing connected neutral-saddle man-ifolds in multi-parameter spatio-temporal stability problems. Its principal advantage is not merely a different evaluation of the eigenvalue derivative, but the replacement of nested saddle searches and parameter-plane boundary reconstruction by direct con-tinuation of the boundary as a solution manifold. This viewpoint provides a natural framework for extending neutral-saddle calculations to larger coupled eigenvalue sys-tems, parameter folds and more complex multi-parameter stability boundaries.
