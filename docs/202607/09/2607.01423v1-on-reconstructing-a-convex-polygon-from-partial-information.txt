Abstract 

The reconstruction problem asks to construct a (convex) polygon that has a specified set of features, such as an ordered set of edge-lengths or an ordered set of polygon-angles. In this paper, we do a systematic exploration of the reconstruction problem in all scenarios where one or two sets of features have been specified. Some of these scenarios were well-studied already, for some we develop testing-algorithms and/or hardness results, and many give rise to interesting open problems for future study. 

# 1 Introduction 

Consider a polygon P with n ≥ 3 corners. This polygon has a number of features , for example the lengths of the n edges, or the n angles at the corners, which can be computed from the coordinates of the corners. In this work, we study the reverse reconstruction question : Given some features of an unknown polygon 

P , is there a realization , i.e., a polygon P that has these features? Can we efficiently test whether there is one? If so, can we construct P efficiently and is it unique (at least up to some obvious transformations)? Questions of this type have been studied previously; we list many results in Appendix A and a few especially relevant ones here. The Minkowski problem asks whether a convex polygon exists that has the origin inside, has a given ordered set of edge lengths ℓ0, . . . , ℓ n−1, and a given ordered set of (unit) edge normals n0, . . . , nn−1, where ni should be the normal of the halfplane supporting the edge of length 

ℓi. See Huang, Yang, and Zhang [ 14 ] for a history and a short proof that this is possible if and only if

triangles spanned by the edges , i.e., the triangles formed by two consecutive corners and the origin? This problem was introduced by Stancu, who called it the L0-Minkowski problem [21 ]. Stancu showed that there always is a solution as long as no two edge normals are opposite to each other. Stancu’s proof is not constructive, and implies no algorithm for computing the desired polygon. We were not able to find a practical algorithm for the L0-Minkowski problem, but inspired by these questions, we decided to explore the problem of reconstructing a convex polygon from features more broadly. Specifically, we looked at six features that naturally arise in any convex polygon with the origin inside, and more generally in any polygon P that is star-shaped from the origin , i.e., all segments from the origin to a corner lie strictly inside P . (We simply call this “star-shaped” here.) Say polygon P has corners c0, . . . , c n−1 in counter-clockwise order. # 2 Preliminaries 

We assume familiarity with basic notation around polygon , convex and star-shaped . When reconstructing a polygon, we always demand that the origin is strictly inside, and disallow flat (180 ◦ degree) polygon angles. Sometimes we consider a convex polygon P that additionally has edge rays hitting their edges , i.e., for all i the ray along edge normal ni actually intersects ei. (This is violated in Figure 1 at ni.) We often use the well known side-area-side-formula (or SAS-formula ), which in our notation means that Ai = 12 didi+1 sin ρi for all indices i. Also, Ai = ℓihi/2. The following will be a helpful tool to reduce the number of scenarios. Any convex polygon P with the origin strictly inside defines another convex polygon P ◦ (the polar polygon with respect to the unit circle ) as follows. Map every corner ( cx, c y ) to the half-plane {(x, y ) : cxx + cy y ≤ 1}, and let P ◦ be the 3intersection of these half-planes. From the definition it follows that the corner rays of P equal the edge rays of P ◦, and furthermore the corner distances of P and the edge distances of P ◦ are inverse to each other. The polar of the polar polygon is the original [ 13 ], and as such, the edge distances of P also equal the inverse corner distances of P ◦. The polar polygon is not well-defined if P is not convex, see also Inchbald [15] for related discussions. 

Input and computer model: Whenever we speak of “given input”, we assume that it satisfies some obviously necessary conditions for there to be a star-shaped polygon. In particular, all areas and lengths must be strictly positive, corner vectors must not all lie in one half-space, and likewise for the edge normals. Also, corner vectors (and for convex polygons also the edge normals) must appear in the circular ccw order around the origin. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, we assume that the input is labelled, i.e., we know exactly which specified length/distance/ray/triangle-area corresponds to which edge/corner. As usual, we assume that the input has been specified with numbers that are small enough to fit into a computer word, and we can do basic arithmetic operations on computer words in constant time (the WordRAM model). This does not permit square roots or trigonometric functions to be computed in constant time, so occasionally we need the more powerful RealRAM model (which permits these [ 19 ,Section 2.3]). 

# 3 Settings Defined by One Type of Information 

We begin with the scenarios where only one feature is specified. Many results are known here already, but to our surprise some appear to be unstudied. 

## 3.1 Known results 

1. Consider the scenario “only edge normals”, which is equivalent to being given polygon angles (up to rotation). Reconstructing a polygon from its polygon angles appears to first have been studied by Vijayan [ 22 ], who proved that any sequence of polygon angles can be realized as long as they sum to ( n − 2)180 ◦.See also [7] and [8] for further results. 2. The scenario “only corner vectors” is the same as the previous, since the edge rays of a convex polygon P equal the corner rays of polar polygon P ◦.3. The scenario “only edge lengths” is closely related to linkage reconfiguration problems. Lenhart and Whitesides [ 16 ] showed that edge lengths can be realized by a convex polygon if and only if the longest edge-length is less than the sum of all others. If so, then the edge lengths can even be realized with all vertices on a common circle [18].

## 3.3 Edge Distance 

Consider the scenario where we are only given the edge distances h0, . . . , h n−1. The following theorem (which rephrases h0, . . . , h n−1 as radii of concentric circles) shows that there always exists a star-shaped realization. 

Theorem 2 For any given sequence of n concentric circles R0, . . . , R n−1 there exists a star-shaped polygon 

P with edges e0, . . . , e n−1 such that for all i the supporting line of ei is tangent to Ri.

Proof. The proof is by induction on n. We prove the stronger claim that all vertices of P lie strictly outside of all circles. For the base case n = 3, assume that R0 has maximum radius. A vertical line tangent to R0 on the right, a horizontal line tangent to R2 on the bottom and a slanted line tangent to 

R1 clearly suffice to define P as desired if we choose the slope of the slanted line very small or large as needed to get all vertices outside of R0. See Figure 3. Assume now that the theorem holds for n = k − 1. If all radii are the same then we draw the polygon as a regular polygon and scale it such that all edges are tangent to the common circle. Otherwise, we may assume that R1 is one of the circles with minimal radius and that the radii of R0, R1 and R2 are not all the same. We also may assume that the radius R0 is not smaller than the radius of R2, otherwise we reverse the order of circles and mirror the polygon in the end. Consequently, the radius of R0 is strictly larger than the radius of R1. By the induction hypothesis we can realize a star-shaped polygon P ′, whose edges are tangent to R0, R 2, . . . , R k−1. We show how to modify P ′ to get to P . To add an edge e1 for the omitted circle R1, we look at the adjacent edges e0 and e2 in P ′ and their common endpoint p. There are two cases based on whether the corner at p is convex or reflex; see Figure 3. In both cases, we shorten e0

(to a new corner r), lengthen e2 (to a new corner q), and ensure that rq lies on a line l1 tangent to R1

with the origin on the correct side for star-shapedness. More specifically, pick a point r in the interior of 

e0, but close enough to p so that it (like p) is outside all circles. Let l1 be the line tangent to R1 and through r; in the convex case we take the one that has the origin and p on the same side, while in the reflex case we take the one that separates the origin from p. Note that this tangent is different from the supporting line of e0, since R1 has strictly smaller radius than R0. Also, the intersection point q between 

l1 and the supporting line of e2 is arbitrarily close to p if we pick r sufficiently close to p; in particular we can pick r such that q is outside all circles as well. One verifies that in both cases the polygon obtained by re-routing via r and q is star-shaped, see also Figure 3. With this the induction step is proved. □

# 4 Settings Defined by Two Types of Information 

## 4.1 Existing or Trivial Results 

Quite a few of the scenarios where two types of features are given have either been studied before, or are trivial. 1. Reconstructing a polygon from edge lengths and edge normals is the Minkowski Problem discussed in the introduction. 2. Reconstructing a polygon from triangle areas and edge normals is the L0-Minkowski problem discussed in the introduction. 3. Reconstructing from corner vectors and corner distances is trivial, because the given information specifies the corners uniquely. 4. Reconstructing from edge normals and edge distances is trivial, since these specify the lines supporting the edges, and each intersection of two consecutive of these lines defines the corner. 5. Reconstructing from edge lengths and corner distances is trivial, since all three side-lengths of triangle Ti are given. So Ti is unique for all i, and with this so is the realization up to rotation. 

## 4.2 Edge Normal and Corner Vector 

Assume that we are given the sequences of edge normals n0, . . . , nn−1 and corner vectors c0, . . . , cn−1.Recall that this determines the polygon angle θi, which is 180 ◦ minus the ccw angle αi between ni−1 and 

ni.As such, the reconstruction problem in this scenario is very closely related to the so-called Aleksandrov Problem, which asks to reconstruct a polygon P from its corner vectors and polygon angles. Necessary and sufficient conditions (depending on α0, . . . , α n−1 and c0, . . . , cn−1) are known for realizability, see Huang [ 14 ] and the references therein. However, Alexandrov’s problem is not exactly the same as ours, because the polygon angles determine the edge-normals only up to rotation. So while the conditions for Alexandrov’s problem are certainly necessary, it is not clear whether they are also sufficient, i.e., we can reconstruct a polygon P that additionally has the corners exactly on the corner rays. (We cannot simply rotate P to achieve this since this might not match the edge normals.) We therefore develop our own easy necessary and sufficient conditions for realizability, and they can be tested in the WordRAM model. See Appendix D.1 for details. 

Theorem 4 For any input sequences of edge normals and corner vectors, we can test in O(n log 2 n) time in the WordRAM model whether a realization exists. If so, then it is unique up to scaling.

85 Further Results and Outlook 

We briefly consider all remaining scenarios here. The crucial ingredient in Section 4.4 was to find a propagation formula that describes the entire polygon based on the input and a variable x. For some scenarios it is easy to develop such propagation formulas, sometimes even in the WordRAM model, see Section D.1 for the variant for Alexandrov’s problem, and Section D.4.3 for the L0-Minkowski problem. Unfortunately, such a propagation formula is helpful only if we can resolve the resulting constraints for the variable(s) that we started with, something that eludes us for the L0-Minkowski problem. For some scenarios we have not been able to find a propagation formula, because at many steps of such a propagation there are two possible ways to proceed. Such a ‘choice’ can in some variants be exploited to prove hardness of the reconstruction problem. See Appendix D.3 for results for scenarios “corner distance and edge distance” as well as “edge-length and corner vector”. We suspect similar hardness results for the remaining scenarios, but this is left for future studies. Finally, many other reconstruction problems are of interest, using other features and/or dropping the requirement of containing the origin inside or having the origin see all corners.

The literature on reconstruction problems in the plane is quite rich, and there are naturally several results where the input data do not correspond to any of the features (or their combinations) considered in this paper. Snoeyink [ 20 ] studied a reconstruction problem where we are given a polygon P (with a triangulation), the polygon angles, and the so-called cross-ratios (a ratio between edge-lengths at the quadrangles defined by the internal edges). He showed that one can uniquely reconstruct the polygon P , up to scaling and rotation, given this information. Biedl, Durocher and Snoeyink [ 3] focused on a setup where the goal is to reconstruct a simple polygon from different types of data that can be obtained from range scanners, e.g., a set of points on the boundary of the polygon such that each point records the line supporting the edge on which the point lies, or such a set that additionally records a vector perpendicular to the line that points towards the polygon’s interior. Barequet, Gotsman and Sidlesky [ 2 ] considered the problem of reconstructing a planar polygon from its intersections with a collection of arbitrarily-oriented “cutting” lines. Chen and Wang [ 4] considered a polygon reconstruction problem where visibility information is relevant: In this case, the input consists of the sorted vertex sequence along the boundary and, for each vertex, the sequence of angles defined by all other visible vertices. Their reconstruction algorithm is an improvement with respect to the one by Disser, Mihal´ ak and Widmayer [ 7 ]. A famous reconstruction problem involving visibility is that of reconstructing a polygon from its vertex visibility graph. The version closest to our paper is probably the one studied by Coullard and Lubiw [ 6 ]: Given an edge-weighted graph G, is it the visibility graph of a simple polygon with the given weights as Euclidean distances? See also the Computational Geometry Handbook [12, Chapter 34] for other polygon reconstruction problems. Finally we mention that there are many related reconstruction problems in 3D; we refer to a recent paper by Cho and Kim [5] that gives a good overview of the history of 3D problems.

## D.3 Hardness results 

D.3.1 Corner Vector and Area (unlabelled)

For convex realizations, the scenario “corner vectors and edge distance” is equivalent to “edge normal and corner distance”, due to polarity. We will therefore only study the former one.
