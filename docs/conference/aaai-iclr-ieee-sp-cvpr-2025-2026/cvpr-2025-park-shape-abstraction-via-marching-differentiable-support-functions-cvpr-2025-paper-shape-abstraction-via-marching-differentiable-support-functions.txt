# Abstract 

Shape abstraction, simplifying shape representation into a set of primitives, is a fundamental topic in computer vi-sion. The choice of primitives shapes the structure of world understanding, yet achieving both high abstraction accu-racy and versatility remains challenging. In this paper, we introduce a novel framework for shape abstraction utiliz-ing a differentiable support function (DSF), which offers unique advantages in representing a wide range of con-vex shapes with fewer parameters, providing smooth sur-face approximation and enabling differentiable contact fea-tures (gap, point, normal) essential for downstream appli-cations involving contact-related problems. To tackle the associated optimization and combinatorial challenges, we introduce two techniques: differentiable shape parameter-ization and hyperplane-based marching to enhance accu-racy and reduce DSF requirements. We validate our method through experiments demonstrating superior accuracy and efficiency, and showcase its applicability in tasks requiring differentiable contact information. 

# 1. Introduction 

Shape representation is pivotal in computer vision and serves as a fundamental building block for explaining and understanding the world in a computable form. Represen-tation techniques range from classical models like meshes and grids to more recently emerging methods such as im-plicit fields and encoders [37, 46], each selected for their suitability to specific applications. Meanwhile, shape ab-straction simplifies these representations into more manage-able primitives, facilitating a more structured understanding of the world. This is also known to align with how hu-mans perceive and understand scenes by separating them into components [2], and often enables more efficient pro-cessing across a variety of applications, including semantic labeling [49], editing [29], and physics simulation [8]. # 2. Related Works 

## 2.1. Geometry Representation and Abstraction 

In computer vision and graphics, meshes are a standard geometry representation. Various methods have been pro-posed to generate meshes from sensory data, such as RGB images and point clouds, using neural networks [5, 13, 17, 45]. Despite their versatility, obtaining high-quality meshes is challenging, and representing smooth surfaces requires a large number of vertices. Another widely used representa-tion is implicit functions, studied in various contexts based on data sources and applications [4, 20, 21, 28, 32, 34]. While effective for representing continuous objects in a scalable manner, they are difficult to structurally interpret and usability is often limited by their output type. Abstracting geometry into convex components has been researched in the convex decomposition field. Mesh de-composition [25, 47], which decomposes a mesh into con-vex hulls, is widely utilized in physics simulations. Neu-ral network-based methods have been developed to generate convexes based on various primitives [8, 11, 35, 38, 40, 48], though these methods face challenges in terms of generaliz-ability and accuracy. More recently, computational methods using superquadrics have been proposed [22, 23], demon-strating superior representation performance compared to neural networks. These methods progressively extract prim-itives from objects, and the number of components varies depending on the shape of the object. While superquadrics can generalize to shapes beyond cuboids and ellipsoids, they remain restricted to symmetric geometries. The DSF introduced in [19], on the other hand, can represent a broader range of smooth convex shapes than superquadrics, while the abstraction method has not been proposed. 

## 2.2. Contact-Related Problems 

Contact-related problems typically arise in fields such as physics simulation and robotics. These problems are in-herently linked to the shape representation of objects, with computation of contact features between them. While con-tact computation have been extensively studied for con-vex shapes [1, 12, 19, 43], performing such queries for general implicit functions is often challenging due to in-compatibility in output values [8]. As a result, only a limited set of shape pairs, such as sphere-sphere, convex-convex, and SDF-point, have been practically employed for contact-related problems. From a computer vision perspec-tive, this connects with various shape reconstruction and abstraction algorithms. For instance, voxel and SDF re-construction methods have been widely used in tasks like grasping [41, 42] and collision avoidance planning [28, 31]. Also several primitive-based abstraction for sensory inputs [8, 29] has been applied to physics simulations. Recently, leveraging gradients in contact-related prob-lems is gaining attention to improve computational effi-ciency, enabling advancements in areas such as reinforce-ment learning [9], manipulation planning [33], and system identification [10, 14]. However, querying differentiable contact between shapes remains an ongoing research. Stud-ies such as [14, 39] focus on basic geometries like spheres, cones, and boxes. Approximate gradients using sampling [18] or randomized smoothing [30] have also been pro-posed, with natural connections to 3D reconstruction tech-niques in computer vision. In contrast, our work aims to abstract shapes by leveraging DSF, which is a theoretically guaranteed shape class that provides analytic gradients.

# 4. Hyperplane-based Marching 

In this section, we propose a method to abstract arbitrary complex shapes as a set of DSFs. As illustrated in the pipeline in Fig. 5, we first generate the SDF from the sen-sory inputs, using computational or learning-based methods proposed in various studies [20, 21, 32, 34]. Consequently, we can obtain a set of SDF data: point ( P ), corresponding SDF value ( S), and gradient of SDF ( G). One naive approach to generating DSFs using these SDF data is to extract a mesh using the marching cube algorithm [24], decompose it into convex meshes [25, 47], and then fit the DSFs. However, the fitting error from Eq. (5) may ac-cumulate throughout the entire process. Furthermore, this approach may yield an excessive number of convex com-ponents since certain geometries that are misaligned with the principal axis [36] cannot be effectively decomposed by axis-aligned cutting planes [47]. In contrast, the marching scheme , iteratively generating the primitives within the ob-ject, can effectively handle the accumulation of fitting er-ror and can intuitively abstract the misaligned geometries. Therefore, we design a marching method for DSF to reduce the number of convex components while improving the rep-resentation performance. A recent study [23] aligns with our problem of interest, proposing a method for marching superquadrics on a given truncated SDF. This approach gradually identifies a col-lection of voxelized distances and extracts primitives from them. However, such voxel-based marching is unsuitable for our framework, as distance data is not readily com-putable from the DSF. Instead, we introduce a hyperplane-based marching approach, which identifies a collection of hyperplanes and fits the DSF within the convex region (in-tersection of halfspaces) based on the formulation described in Eq. (5), progressively filling the occupied space. To

## 4.4. Hybrid Method and Self-Degeneration 

Although the region reduction method has a high represen-tation performance, its strict exclusion of exterior regions may result in an excessive number of DSFs for highly con-cave geometries. While the region expansion method can neglect minor concavities based on the weighted average distance formulation in Eq. (15), reducing the number of DSFs for highly concave geometries, it may also result in a loss of surface detail. To address these limitations, we propose a hybrid method that balances representation per-formance with the number of convex components. Initially, Alg. 1 is used to produce DSFs with high rep-resentation power. If the minimum SDF value exceeds a threshold ts, Alg. 2 is applied to efficiently cover the re-maining parts. The details of the hybrid method are de-scribed in Alg. 3. The comparison among the three meth-ods is visualized in Fig. 7. After fitting each DSF to R,DSFs with small volumes or significant discrepancies from the SDF are degenerated. The details of the degeneration process are provided in the supplementary material. 

# 5. Experiments 

## 5.1. Abstraction Results 

Our DSF abstraction method using hyperplane-based marching (MDSF, Alg. 3) is compared with three differ-ent baselines: an optimization method based on a neural network, a fitting method, and a marching method. The optimization method based on a neural network is imple-mented by CvxNet [8] trained for a single object, optimiz-ing all primitives simultaneously. The fitting method is im-plemented by the state-of-the-art mesh decomposition algo-rithm (CoACD [47]), and by fitting the DSF to each convex mesh from CoACD (CoDSF). For the marching method, the state-of-the-art marching algorithm for superquadrics (MP [23]) is compared with our method. For CvxNet [8], the batch size and the maximum training steps are set to 32 and 5000, respectively. For CoDSF and our method, we set Nv in Eq. (2) and Nh in Eq. (5) to 20 and 1742, respectively. The overlap parameter od in Alg. 1 is set to a large value, greater than the object size, to allow intersection between DSFs. The evaluation is conducted on the YCB dataset [3] and the robot links CAD data provided by the MuJoCo Menagerie [50], including 5 robots with a total of 51 links and 14 objects from YCB. The comparison metrics are intersection over union (IoU), Chamfer-L1 distance, and the number of primitives ( Nc). Definitions for IoU and Chamfer-L1 distance are provided in the supplementary material. IoU for DSF is calculated by determining occu-pancy for each point sample using the minimum distance solver for DSF [1]. For Chamfer-L1 distance, the surface points of superquadrics are sampled via the equal-distance sampling method in [22], and the surface points of DSF are obtained from a fine mesh derived from the DSF. The abstraction results are visualized in Fig. 8. The re-sults of IoU, Chamfer-L1, and Nc are summarized in Tab. 1. Our DSF abstraction produces higher IoU, lower Chamfer-L1, and lower Nc than all other baselines, which demon-strates abstraction efficiency: higher representation power with fewer primitives. The visualizations for the YCB dataset [3] are provided in the supplementary material.

# 7. Discussions and Conclusions 

We propose two techniques for DSF-based shape abstrac-tion: the differentiable shape parameterization and the hyperplane-based marching. The differentiable shape pa-rameterization enables the efficient and robust DSF fitting across a wide range of convex geometries, providing differ-entiable contact features with respect to the shape parame-ters. For complex geometries, the hyperplane-based march-ing method improves abstraction accuracy and reduces the required number of DSF components. Our method out-performs the state-of-the-art in terms of accuracy and effi-ciency, achieving higher accuracy with fewer components. Additionally, we provide three applications that leverage the differentiable contact features of abstracted DSFs: shape editing, contact localization, and differentiable simulation. Although our current approach relies on the SDF data, additional visual information such as texture embeddings and differentiable rendering can be utilized through the dif-ferentiable formulations of DSF. This integration has the potential to enhance both accuracy and applicability across a range of computer vision tasks in future works.
